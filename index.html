Section 1

The Heat Is Rising Across Australia

Average maximum temperatures across Australia have climbed steadily from 2008 to 2017. The national average peaked at 25°C in 2017 — nearly 2.2°C above the 2012 low of 22.3°C. This warming trend creates the dry, combustible conditions that fuel bushfires.

Average Maximum Temperature Across Australia, 2008–2017
National average of daily maximum temperatures across all 49 weather stations · Source: Kaggle / BOM Weather Dataset
Key Finding

The sharpest rise occurred between 2012 and 2014 — a 1.5°C jump in just two years. The years 2013–2014 also coincided with severe drought conditions across eastern Australia.

Section 2

The North Burns Hottest. The Interior Dries Out.

Not all of Australia heats equally. Northern stations like Katherine and Darwin average above 32°C year-round, while inland stations like Woomera and Uluru see almost no rainfall — less than 1mm per day on average. These are the fire-risk heartlands.

Section 3

Where the Fires Are Burning Right Now

Satellite data from NASA MODIS (March–June 2026) shows 27,566 high-confidence fire detections across Australia. Each point represents a fire detected from space, sized by its Fire Radiative Power (FRP) — a measure of heat intensity. The map reveals fire clusters concentrated in Queensland, the Northern Territory, and Western Australia.

Active Fire Detections Across Australia — NASA MODIS Satellite Data (2026)
Each point is a confirmed fire detection with confidence ≥ 50%. Size encodes Fire Radiative Power (FRP). Colour shows date — earlier fires in darker orange, recent in bright red. · Source: NASA FIRMS
Reading the Map

The densest clusters appear in northern Queensland and the Northern Territory — regions that combine extreme heat (above 32°C average) with seasonal dry conditions. The high-intensity fires (largest points) are concentrated in the sparsely populated interior.

Section 4

Which Stations Are Most at Risk?

Combining high temperatures, low humidity, and low rainfall identifies the stations at greatest fire risk. Woomera, Alice Springs, Cobar and Mildura sit at the extreme end — hot, dry and increasingly fire-prone as the climate shifts.
