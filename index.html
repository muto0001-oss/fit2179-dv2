Australia Burns

Australia is one of the most fire-prone continents on Earth. This visualisation explores where, when, and how intensely bushfires have shaped the land — and the lives of people who call it home.

01 — Geography

Where Australia Burns

Fire risk is not evenly distributed. The Northern Territory sees the largest total area burned annually due to its vast tropical savannas and dry seasons. New South Wales and Victoria face fewer but far more destructive fires, driven by drought conditions and extreme summer heat.

02 — History

Seasons of Fire, 2009–2023

The total area burned each season has been volatile, punctuated by catastrophic outlier years. The 2019–20 Black Summer dwarfs every other season on record.

The 2019–20 Black Summer burned over 24 million hectares — more than any previously recorded season — and generated smoke that circled the entire globe.

03 — State Breakdown

Black Summer: The Human and Physical Cost

New South Wales bore the greatest burden during 2019–20 — the most homes destroyed, the most direct deaths, and the largest forested area burned. The Northern Territory burned more total land but its remoteness meant far fewer lives and structures were lost.

04 — Fire Management

Planned Burns vs. Uncontrolled Bushfire

Not all fire is destruction. Planned (prescribed) burns are deliberately lit to reduce fuel loads and protect communities. States like the Northern Territory use planned fire extensively. During the 2019–20 season, however, unplanned fires dominated in the south-east — a sign that conditions overwhelmed fire management capacity.

05 — Human Cost

Does More Fire Mean More Deaths?

Larger fires do not always produce more fatalities. Emergency response systems, population density, and fire behaviour all shape the human toll. Black Saturday 2009 killed 173 people from a relatively smaller fire, while the much larger Black Summer of 2019–20 claimed 33 direct lives — a testament to improved warnings and evacuation systems, yet still devastating in scale.
